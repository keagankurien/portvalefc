Social Media Audit · May 2026

Port Vale FC

"To be a successful club, on and off the pitch, which is at the heart of our community."

Port Vale's social presence is grounded, community-led, and visually coherent. Those things take longer to build than a follower count and they are genuinely present here. The channels do not shout. The content does not oversell. The opportunity is not a change of direction. It is giving the existing identity more to work with, and using the channels to keep supporters close through a difficult season in League Two.

Instagram
Primary visual channel · kit photography · community storytelling
+
Tone of voice
Restrained and honest. Captions do not oversell. At its best, it reads like it was written by someone who actually goes to Vale Park on a Saturday.
Visual style
Valiant Black (#20201e) and Valiant Amber (#fab500) hold consistently across content types. Kit photography is close-framed, tactile, and premium for League One level.
Content themes
Kit drops · matchday · player features · World Cup call-ups · @supervaleabroad integration · kit and commercial content.
What's working
Visual consistency is rare at this level and it's real here. The @supervaleabroad World Cup tagging shows communications layering beyond standard posting, player, sponsor, and supporter group in one post.
What could improve
Vale Park itself, the physical atmosphere, the terraces, the matchday texture, is almost entirely absent. It's the most distinctive visual asset the club has, and it barely appears on the feed.
Opportunity
Document Vale Park the way the kit photography documents the shirt: close, considered, and specific. Look beyond Burslem too. Fan creators and football content accounts based internationally, including in the US where the @supervaleabroad community already exists, are an underused route to new audiences. A collaboration with a visiting football creator would reach people the club's own feed never will.

X / Twitter
Real-time matchday channel · where belief builds during a promotion run
+
Tone of voice
Functional and direct. Consistent with the wider brand voice. Understated rather than excitable, which suits the club and should be kept that way.
Visual style
Brand colour consistency carries across to result templates. Amber-black identity reads correctly without being forced.
Content themes
Match updates · results · team news · commercial announcements · player milestones.
What's working
The voice is consistent and on-brand. Doesn't perform excitement the moment doesn't call for. The amber-black identity holds without being forced across different content types.
What could improve
After relegation, the temptation is to go quiet. It is usually the wrong call. Supporters want to feel the club is still communicating and still believes in what comes next.
Opportunity
In a rebuilding season, X is where supporter belief gets managed day to day. Honest, consistent posting around match reactions, small wins, and community moments keeps people close during a difficult year.
TikTok
Audience growth · youth engagement · promotion momentum
+
Tone of voice
Looser than Instagram. Player personality and behind-the-scenes access land better here. The club's people-centred values translate naturally into TikTok's preferred register.
Visual style
Authenticity-first, appropriate for the platform. Amber-black identity should be present in framing without dominating the format or making it feel like a branded asset.
Content themes
Player features, matchday prep, dressing room access, skills clips, fan journey content. A League Two season still generates compelling short-form material. Resilience, community, and the characters inside a club are exactly what TikTok audiences respond to.
What's working
Port Vale has the personality and the stories. The community-first identity is a genuine TikTok asset. Not every club has it. The ground, the town, and the people are all there waiting to be shown.
What could improve
No consistent posting rhythm visible from the outside. Without a repeatable format, growth comes in one-off spikes rather than the steady habit-building that actually moves the follower count.
Opportunity
A weekly matchday behind-the-scenes format, low cost, high authenticity, would be a direct pipeline from the promotion narrative into shareable content.
Facebook
Community channel · established supporter base · longer-form content
+
Tone of voice
Consistent with the wider brand voice. Comment culture from supporters is active, though the club's participation in those threads appears limited. Listening more would cost nothing.
Visual style
Content largely cross-posted from other channels. The amber-black palette holds by default. No Facebook-native formatting or approach is evident.
Content themes
Match results · kit and commercial announcements · community foundation activity. Facebook is the natural home for foundation coverage given the demographic.
What's working
The loyal supporter base on Facebook is exactly the audience for community stories and heritage content. Reach is lower. Intent is high.
What could improve
No Facebook-native content strategy. Longer-form video, match recaps, and community foundation features are not being used on the platform that suits them best.
Opportunity
A monthly community foundation feature, written for Facebook, not cropped from Instagram, would be low cost and high relevance with this audience.
YouTube
Long-form content, fan journeys, creator partnerships
+
Tone of voice
Documentary and editorial when at its best. The club's values, tenacious, people-centred, valiant, translate naturally into long-form video storytelling.
Visual style
Applying the amber-black palette consistently to thumbnails would improve discoverability without additional production spend. A simple template would do it.
Content themes
Match highlights, player and manager interviews, academy content, community stories, fan journey documentaries. A Port Vale supporter from the USA travelling to Burslem to watch their first ever live match is exactly the kind of content that earns views well outside the existing fanbase.
What's working
Academy pathways, community work, Vale Park's physical character, genuinely compelling long-form material that only this club has access to. The raw stories are strong.
What could improve
Without a recurring format, YouTube builds view spikes rather than subscriber habits. A reason for people to return each week beyond match recaps is missing.
Opportunity
A US-based supporter's first trip to Vale Park, documented from the airport to the final whistle, is exactly the kind of video that travels globally. It costs almost nothing to produce and earns far more reach than any match highlight will. Partnering with football content creators in this space, ground-hopping accounts, lower-league YouTube channels, travel vlogs through football, amplifies it further. The community identity is the hook. It is already there.
LinkedIn
B2B channel · community foundation · commercial partnerships
+
Tone of voice
Professional. The community mission and 150-year heritage are strong B2B positioning in the Stoke-on-Trent business community. It should read like the club's values translated into a business context, not like a press release.
Visual style
Brand guidelines provide clear corporate templates. The amber-black palette should apply consistently to LinkedIn graphics to maintain the visual identity across all channels.
Content themes
Partnership announcements · community impact · Academy employer links · commercial opportunities. Sponsor storytelling belongs here more than on any other channel.
What's working
"The EFL's community club" is a differentiated B2B position in the local market. Sponsors want association with genuine community work. LinkedIn is where that earns the relationships, not just the logo placement.
What could improve
The gap between what the club actually does in the community and what it communicates about it is the biggest missed opportunity in the portfolio. The stories exist. They are just not being told here.
Opportunity
One community impact post per month, one story, one partner, real outcomes. That is how you build commercial relationships before the conversation even starts.
Standards
What good looks like
  • Photography that treats Vale Park as a character, not just a backdrop
  • Supporter stories that go beyond what happened on the pitch. The people who show up every week are the story.
  • Partnership content that explains what the relationship actually means, not just where the logo sits
  • Content written for Facebook and LinkedIn specifically, not the same post pushed to every channel
  • A rebuild narrative that is honest about the situation and specific about what comes next
  • Copy that is direct and honest. Port Vale supporters do not need the club to oversell anything to them.
Recommendations
Four practical moves
01
Document Vale Park properly. Apply the same photographic care to the ground that the kit content gets. The light on the terraces, the matchday atmosphere. Nobody else has that ground.
02
Go deeper on sponsor storytelling. Kit posts show where the logo sits. A short feature on what a partnership actually means to the club earns more goodwill and makes sponsors feel the relationship is worth renewing.
03
Partner with football content creators. Ground-hopping accounts, lower-league YouTube channels, and fan journey creators are a low-cost route to audiences the club's own feed will never reach on its own.
04
Post monthly community impact content on LinkedIn. One story, one partner, real outcomes. That is how you build the next commercial relationship before the conversation even begins.
Looking ahead
Relegation is a content moment too, if the channels handle it right
League Two · 2025–26 · the rebuild starts now
How a club handles relegation on social media tells its supporters a lot about what kind of club it actually is.
The instinct after relegation is to post less and say less. Wait for better news. The clubs that come back well tend to do the opposite. They lean into the community. They show the work. They give supporters a reason to stay engaged before the results arrive to justify it. Port Vale's stated mission is to be at the heart of the community. That is not a difficult brief to write content for. The channels need to be honest about where the club is and specific about what the rebuild looks like. Fan content, creator partnerships, Vale Park properly documented, the @supervaleabroad community kept involved. In a difficult season these are not optional extras. They are how you keep people believing.
Relegation tests what a club's social channels are actually for. Port Vale's answer is the right one: community, honesty, and being specific about the place and the people. I hope the team keeps showing up that way, on the pitch and behind the scenes.
